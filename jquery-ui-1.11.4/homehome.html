News About Women In Games



FIFA 16: Breaking Barriers in (Virtual) Sports

In a lovely showcase of progress, EA's new release, FIFA 16 is the first game of its franchise to include the option to play as women. Acknowledging the growing popularity of women's football, especially in national teams, the game now gives every player the option to play as their national men's team or national women's team. Other similar series like Pez, which deals with club football and not national football, have yet to implement this modification, but will hopefully follow suit.

Girls Who Code Wow The World Again With "Bias Breaker" Game

New York high school students Shazmin Mahmud (17) and Jay Pen (15), in association with Girls Who Code, have created Bias Breaker, an RPG-style game in which the player selects one of a diverse range of characters, then proceeds to shoot harmful stereotypes (represented as blobs). Who hasn't wanted to kill stereotypes?

E3 Delivers Some Badass Women

E3 is the biggest gaming event of the year, where triple-A games are announced and demos are shown to a worldwide audience as a promise of what's to come. So how did the big releases do on female characters this years? According to The Mary Sue, not bad at all. They've compiled a list of 23 games featuring kick-ass playable female characters, including titles like Mass Effect: Andromeda (in which you can choose to play as male or female), Rise of the Tomb Raider (featuring the much beloved Lara Croft), Michonne (a spin-off of the highly successful The Walking Dead Game, focused on the story of female character Michonne), and Fable Legends (part of the popular Fable series and featuring multiple playable female characters).

Game Developer's Conference's Panel on Women's Growing Participation eSports

This year's Game Developer's Conference in March featured a wonderful panel on the participation of women in eSports, an area you could almos say is more overwhelmingly male than the rest of the gaming industry. Some of the speakers include Heather Mumm, Rachel Quirico, and Kim Phan. Stigmas, social pressures and the current environment are all discussed by women prominent in gaming.
